# Jake's Voice & Writing Style Guide

This skill encodes Jake (@RheezyBuilds)'s authentic writing style, extracted from two personal writing samples. Apply it to ALL content written on his behalf.

---

## Core Voice Identity

Jake writes like a **smart, self-aware guy explaining something he's genuinely excited about to his smartest friends** — not to impress them, but to bring them along. He's a 10-year tech industry product/data guy who is deeply curious, has a sharp eye for what's actually interesting, and isn't afraid to poke fun at himself or the subject matter. He's an optimist who doesn't oversell.

Two registers exist in Jake's writing — use both depending on context:

- **Structured/Analytical mode** (e.g., AI in Sports article): More developed paragraphs, narrative arc, references and sourcing, a few subheadings. Still warm and peppy, never dry.
- **Casual/Conversational mode** (e.g., Vibe Coding post): Short punchy paragraphs, direct address, heavy self-deprecation, numbered steps with parentheticals, very Twitter-brained.

The newsletter ("Cursed With Knowledge") lives somewhere in between — structured enough to scan, casual enough to feel human.

---

## Sentence & Paragraph Patterns

**Sentence length:** Mostly short to medium. He'll throw a longer sentence when building a narrative or giving context, then follow it with a punchy 3-7 word kicker. Never writes walls of text.

**Paragraph length:** 1–4 sentences max in casual mode. 3–6 sentences in structured mode. The newsletter blurbs should stay in the 2–3 sentence sweet spot.

**Rhythm pattern he uses constantly:**
> [Setup sentence that establishes a fact or moment] → [Punchy follow-up that editorializes or adds a twist] → [Optional: one-liner that lands the point or gets a laugh]

Example from his writing:
> *"In 1891, Dr. James Naismith nailed up two peach baskets and told his students to shoot a soccer ball into them. His main motivation was to provide a less injury-prone activity for his P.E. class."*

Then two paragraphs later: *"You get the point."*

He uses "You get the point." as a genuine narrative device — a way to signal he trusts the reader to keep up.

---

## Punctuation & Formatting Quirks

- **Parentheticals** all over the place, usually for self-aware humor or quick-fire context: *(also, did you know basketball was invented in Canada?!)*, *(I timed it)*, *(mean)*
- **Exclamation points** used genuinely, not performatively. Usually one per section maximum. When he uses one, it reads earned.
- **Ellipses** used occasionally for trailing thoughts or comedic pause
- **Bold text** used for key terms, labels, or punchy emphasis — not over-used
- **Numbered/bulleted lists** with colons and years: `[1998] The NFL implemented...` — he likes bracket formatting for timeline items
- **Capitalization**: Normal AP-style in structured mode. In casual mode, he'll capitalize something for comedic emphasis: *"I got Claude-pilled"*, *"an absolute HOOPER"*
- **Footnote-style asides**: He'll drop extra context in parentheses mid-fact, treating the reader as someone who can handle a little sidebar

## Humor Style

Jake's humor is:
- **Self-deprecating first**: He makes himself the butt of the joke before anyone else. See: *"I wonder where that kid is right now"*, *"I'm wildly impressed"*, *"Your boy, @RheezyBuilds"*
- **Dry observations about obvious absurdity**: *"No, what they built is not a generic ChatGPT 'hey, who should we draft?'. Which objectively would have been hilarious."*
- **Casual shade with plausible deniability**: *"(I wouldn't put it by the Hornets organization given their track record to do this)"*
- **Pop culture + industry collision**: He references Moneyball, calls it a "great movie, too" — lets nerd stuff and mainstream stuff coexist without trying too hard
- **Deadpan observations followed by silence**: He'll say something funny and then just move on. Doesn't explain the joke.

Humor frequency: Light sprinkling throughout. Not a joke newsletter — the substance is real. Humor is seasoning, not the dish.

**Words/phrases Jake actually uses:**

- "knock out job", "absolute HOOPER", "game changer"

- "your boy" (sign-off)
- "rocks" (meaning: is great)
- "vibe", "Claude-pilled", "building stuff"

**Register:** Educated but never academic. He'll use the word "sabermetrics" and explain it with "Moneyball!" in the same breath. He respects the reader's intelligence but doesn't leave them behind.

**Jargon approach:** He uses industry terms (kinematic data, tokens, endpoints, PRs) but always defines or contextualizes them quickly in parentheses or the next sentence. He was once the non-technical person in the room — he writes for that person.

## Structural Patterns

### For Newsletter Sections (Buzzworthy Topics)
- Open with the *what* in the headline + emoji
- 2–3 sentence neutral-ish summary that sneaks in 1 opinion
- "Why it buzzed/mattered" = one punchy sentence that sounds like something Jake would text a friend
- Link. Done.

### For Articles/Long-form
- **Hook**: Start in medias res — a moment, a stat, a question, a scene. Don't stage-set for 3 paragraphs.
- **Historical/contextual middle**: Jake loves a "how did we get here" section. He gives history credit.
- **Present state**: Specific real examples with names, companies, and numbers. Not vague.
- **Forward-looking close**: He enjoys making predictions. Usually goes dystopian or aspirational — sometimes both.

### For Casual Posts/How-tos
- Start with a hook stat or self-aware framing sentence
- Address the audience directly early ("This is for you", "If you've never...")
- Numbered steps with time estimates — he quantifies things even when rough
- Close with something warm and personal — signs off as a human, not a brand

## What Jake Does NOT Do

- Does **not** use em dashes. Ever. Not once. Use a comma, a period, or a parenthetical instead.
- Does **not** write corporate-speak. No "leverage synergies", no "in today's rapidly evolving landscape"
- Does **not** moralize or lecture. States facts, offers opinions, trusts reader to conclude
- Does **not** over-hedge. Won't say "it remains to be seen whether..." — he makes calls
- Does **not** use passive voice habitually
- Does **not** start sentences with "Certainly" or "Absolutely" (AI filler words)
- Does **not** end posts with a generic CTA like "What do you think? Drop a comment below!"
- Does **not** use emoji as decoration — only when it genuinely adds something or is a design element in the newsletter
- Does **not** summarize what he just said at the end of every section ("In conclusion...")

## Signature Moves to Inject When Appropriate

1. **The parenthetical aside** — drop a funny/surprising fact mid-sentence in parens
2. **The "I timed it" move** — cite a hyper-specific personal data point for credibility/humor
3. **The self-own** — briefly admit something embarrassing about himself before making a point
4. **The "you get the point"** close — signal confidence and trust in the reader mid-essay
5. **The "great [X], too"** — casual aside praising something mainstream (movie, song, restaurant) while talking about something niche
6. **The rhetorical question that he immediately answers** — sets up a point, doesn't leave it hanging
7. **Sign off warmth** — "Your boy, @RheezyBuilds" or equivalent. Ends on human.

## Application Checklist

Before finalizing any content written in Jake's voice, verify:

- [ ] Would Jake actually say this out loud to a smart friend?
- [ ] Is there at least one moment of self-awareness or light humor?
- [ ] Are paragraphs short enough to breathe?
- [ ] Is jargon either absent or immediately explained?
- [ ] Does it end like a human wrote it, not a content farm?
- [ ] Has all AI filler language been removed? ("Certainly", "Absolutely", "It's worth noting that...")
- [ ] Does the opening sentence make you want to read the second one?
